Abstract
Chest Pneumonia Detection is an AI-driven web application developed to assist in the
early diagnosis of pneumonia through the classification of chest X-ray images using deep
learning techniques. The application employs a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
trained on the COVID-19 Radiography Database available on Kaggle, which includes
thousands of labeled X-ray images across categories such as normal, pneumonia,
lung-opacity and COVID-19.
The CNN model has been fine-tuned through preprocessing techniques like resizing,
normalization, and data augmentation to enhance its accuracy and generalization. This
enables the system to provide fast and reliable classification results, supporting medical
practitioners and users in identifying pneumonia cases without the need for advanced
radiological infrastructure.
As a web-based application, Chest Pneumonia Detection offers platform-independent
access, allowing users to upload chest X-ray images and instantly receive diagnostic
predictions. Built with a focus on usability and accessibility, the system is particularly
beneficial for remote or underserved regions where expert radiologists and diagnostic tools
may be unavailable.
By integrating deep learning with the COVID-19 Radiography Database and delivering it
through a web platform, the application demonstrates the potential of artificial intelligence to
improve diagnostic efficiency, enhance healthcare accessibility, and support timely
clinical decision-making in the detection of pneumonia and related conditions.

Chapter 1: introduction
1.1 Overview
In recent years, the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) in healthcare has
transformed the way medical diagnostics are approachedâ€”enhancing accuracy, speed,
and accessibility. Chest Pneumonia Detection is a web-based diagnostic tool designed
to support the early identification of pneumonia through the analysis of chest X-ray
images using deep learning.
The application leverages a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on the
publicly available COVID-19 Radiography Database from Kaggle, which contains
thousands of chest X-ray images labeled as normal, pneumonia, and COVID-19. This
model enables the system to deliver fast, reliable classification results, helping healthcare

Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI that focuses on developing algorithms that allow
computers to learn from data and make predictions or decisions without being explicitly
programmed. Instead of relying on predefined rules, ML systems identify patterns in data and
improve their performance over time through experience [2].
Types of Machine Learning Algorithms:

with a corresponding output.
o Purpose: To predict outputs for new, unseen inputs based on the learned
patterns.
o Common Algorithms:

trees to improve accuracy and reduce overfitting [6].
ď‚§ Support Vector Machines (SVMs): Finds the optimal hyperplane to
separate data into classes, particularly effective for high-dimensional
data [7].
ď‚§ Neural Networks: Mimics the structure of the human brain to solve
complex tasks, such as image recognition and natural language
processing [8].

o Definition: The algorithm learns patterns and structures from unlabeled data.
o Purpose: To explore the data or group it into clusters based on similarities.

5.3 Results and Discussion
Model Overview:
In this project, we developed a custom Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model to
classify chest X-ray images into four categories: Normal, COVID, Viral Pneumonia, and
Lung Opacity. The CNN was built using the Keras Sequential API with layers such as
Conv2D, MaxPooling2D, BatchNormalization, Dropout, and Dense. All input images were
resized to 128Ă—128 grayscale format to standardize dimensions and reduce computational
load.
In addition to the CNN, we implemented an ensemble learning model that combines the
predictions of multiple deep learning architectures to improve robustness and generalization.
The ensemble included models such as ResNet18, DenseNet121, and GoogLeNet. These
models were chosen due to their strong performance in image classification tasks and their
complementary learning capabilities. The ensemble approach used majority voting and was
trained on the same dataset as the CNN for comparative evaluation.
Training Details:
The CNN was trained using the Adam optimizer, which is well-suited for problems with
sparse gradients and dynamic learning rates. We employed callbacks such as EarlyStopping
and ReduceLROnPlateau to avoid overfitting and to fine-tune the training process. Image
augmentation techniques like rotation, zooming, flipping, and shifting were applied to enrich
the dataset and improve model generalizability.
The ensemble model was trained on the same preprocessed and augmented dataset. Each
base learner contributed to the final prediction through a soft-voting mechanism. While the
ensemble achieved decent performance, it did not surpass the CNN model.
Performance Metrics:
The CNN model achieved a training accuracy of 94% and a testing accuracy of 85%, as
indicated by the classification report and confusion matrix. The ensemble model, by
comparison, achieved slightly lower performance metrics, with training accuracy around
91% and testing accuracy at 83%.
The classification report for the CNN showed higher precision and recall values across most
categories. The confusion matrix demonstrated a strong diagonal pattern, indicating that the
CNN made accurate predictions with minimal confusion between classes.

Conclusion
Our investigation revealed that maintaining respiratory health is one of the most pressing
global health challenges. In light of this, we focused on detecting and classifying chest X-ray
images, particularly targeting COVID-19, viral pneumonia, and normal lung conditions using
deep learning.
Early and accurate diagnosis of chest-related conditionsâ€”especially during pandemicsâ€”is
critical to prevent misdiagnosis, reduce the burden on healthcare systems, and provide timely
treatment. Delay in diagnosis can result in worsening of symptoms, longer hospitalization
periods, and even fatal complications. Hence, reliable chest X-ray classification plays a vital
role in aiding doctors and improving patient outcomes.
This project proposes a machine learning-based approach for automatic classification of
chest X-ray images. This tool is intended to support medical professionals by reducing
diagnosis time and enhancing accuracy, while also enabling the potential for self-assessment
tools for public use.
Initially, we explored building an ensemble model using multiple deep learning architectures.
However, the ensemble approach did not produce satisfactory results in terms of accuracy.
After further experimentation, we shifted our focus to Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNN), which delivered significantly better performance.
We used the COVID-19 Radiography Database from Kaggle (link), which includes a diverse
set of labeled chest X-ray images across categories such as COVID-19, Viral Pneumonia,
and Normal.
After applying preprocessing and data augmentation techniques (rotation, zoom, and
flipping), the dataset contained approximately 34,000 images. We divided the data into 80%
for training, 10% for validation, and 10% for testing.
We used a CNN model with optimized hyperparameters and the Adam optimizer (learning
rate = 0.0001). After several iterations, the model achieved an accuracy of 94%, significantly
outperforming our earlier ensemble attempts.
